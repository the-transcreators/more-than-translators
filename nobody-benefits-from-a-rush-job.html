Nobody benefits from a rush job

Translators loath them for good reasons – the rush jobs. To set the scene, it’s Thursday evening, or even worse, Friday morning when a client sends out work that needs to be translated by Monday. For fear of losing revenue, or even worse a client, the translator agrees to deliver the translation by the requested deadline. If it’s a manageable job it won’t be an issue.  However, if it’s a bigger job that warrants more time to deliver quality work it will most probably result in, you guessed it, a rush job. The translator ends up spending the weekend sweating over large volumes of text with the looming deadline in mind and often delivers substandard or even inaccurate copy simply to get the job done on time. Neither client nor translator benefit from a rush job.

Why do rush jobs happen?

Rush jobs usually happen when translations are not seen as an integral part of a project and limited time and thought are given to them at the planning stage, or when a translator is set an unrealistic deadline due to a lack of understanding of how long a transcreated piece of copy takes. 

What can be done to avoid rush jobs and improve quality?

Some simple steps can be taken to avoid rush jobs and improve quality. Firstly, clients need to understand the importance of allocating time for translations at the project planning stage. This point is often overlooked or underestimated. Secondly, clients need to involve translators more in the planning process so realistic lead times for quality translations can be agreed upon. Finally, clients need to be available to answer questions if they arise during the transcreation process, which is rarely the case with rush jobs.
