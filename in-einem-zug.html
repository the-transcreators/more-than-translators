Would you translate this as 'In a train to Tirol'?

I have seen this sign many times and have always admired the excellent wordplay. As far as I know there is no English version of this ad, and I don't think there needs to be one. I'm pretty sure it is aimed primarily at German visitors to Tirol in Austria.

I'm going to try to transcreate it into English anyway, just for fun, and as a quick example of the transcreation process.

Right. What's the message?

  • We encourage you to come to Tirol by train instead of by car
  • Coming to Tirol by train is quick
  • You can go directly, even from Germany

The word-for-word translation 'In a train to Tirol' is, of course, not a proper translation. It doesn't even transfer one of the two elements of the message contained in the German wordplay. In fact, in English it comes across as no more than a fairly pointless statement. It just hangs in the air. A normal translator might still be tempted to use it though. You can understand why if you remember that regular, jobbing translators are mostly paid per word, and as little per word as the customer or agency can get away with. On top of that, they are usually not provided with the context needed to do a proper job.

It is clear that what makes the message stick in your head is the excellent wordplay. It reverberates in the mind. Can we find an equivalent in English? Well, although you could translate the German expression in einem Zug into English as 'in one fell swoop' or '(all) in one go', neither is usable in this context.

Oh dear.

If we don't have a wordplay, what can we do instead that would be similar in its effect on the reader?

We could use a different literary device. Alliteration, for example, rather than wordplay. I have one idea. I don't like it as much as the source, but it would work, and, importantly, it is usable for the intended purpose, and it contains both elements of the message:

'Take the train straight to Tirol'
This idea would work.

Maybe if a driver saw that a few times on a stretch of road (there are several of these ads on billboards placed along certain sections of trunk road), it might start off a brainworm (a brainworm is my own term for something that gets stuck in your head a bit like an earworm, except that it's a phrase rather than a piece of music). Hmm... I'm starting to like it more. If you say it a few times to yourself, the rhythm starts to sound like a train clacking along the track.

It's good to like your own ideas... but not too much!

So I asked Warren (the other author on this blog) if he had any ideas. Without thinking about it too much, he suggested:

'Non-stop all the way to Tirol'
This idea would NOT work.

Yes, it seems very good at first glance. Actually, both of our initial reactions were that this was better than my idea. Then we quickly realized that it was unusable. Whether or not it is really non-stop depends on where you start your journey. One of the cleverest things about the German original is that it uses the established expression in einem Zug to imply that it will be non-stop without making any impossible claims. You understand that the trip will be quick, but you will accept that you might need to change trains at some point in the journey. And for German visitors, the message that trains run directly from Germany into Austria is still clear. Wonderful stuff!

That's a great illustration of how we work (whether together or separately). We throw any and all ideas into the air, but we do not fall in love with any of them. We think things all the way through to the end. And we never forget the power of the word. The power to get a message across clearly. Or the power to mislead and confuse.

If you are from the company 'Tirol Info', then I would like to say that you have my sincere admiration for a great strapline in German. And although my English idea is probably not quite as good, I still claim copyright to it. So if you want to use it, please get in touch first.

And whoever you are, if you can think of something better, I'd love to know what it is. Just out of interest.

So, there's a quick and realistic example of what transcreation is all about. And it's rather fun, isn't it?
