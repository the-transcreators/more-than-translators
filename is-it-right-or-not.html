Is it right? Or isn't it? How can you be sure?

It is fairly safe to assume that pretty much everyone who graduated from high school in the last 20 to 30 years can speak and write at least a little English. In our globalized world, English has become the primary tool for international communication in business, science, education and more. Although English might not be the most spoken first language, it is by far the most widely spoken second language.

With so many non-native speakers, it is no surprise that some of them can write English so well that on a quick read-through almost nobody would know that it was not written by a native speaker. However, on closer inspection—and when it is important to fully understand what is meant—the cracks begin to show. That isn't surprising. It is very difficult indeed to work with a reduced set of tools in the communicational toolbox. For all but a very small number of extremely talented individuals, it is almost "mission impossible".

If it is so difficult to write well in a foreign language—and it is—why do people go to all that effort in order to produce a result that they can never be sure is right? In fact, they can be almost certain that it isn't quite right, but they can't see where. We all have a reduced sensitivity to the quality and precise meaning of written material in other languages. How can anyone—apart from the talented few—even expect to be sure that what they have written in another language is as good as it needs to be to do what it needs to do? Short answer: they can't. I can't. That is the reality.

It is also the reality that in many cases the writer first writes a piece in their own language, then attempts to translate it into English. They think they should easily be able to do it because they wrote the source text and they speak English every day at work. But now they are making things even more difficult. Not only do they have a limited communicational toolbox in English, and can never be quite sure if something is right or not, now they are also trying to translate a piece of text that they themselves are particularly close to. When they wrote it, they became fond of the imagery they used and will find it hard to accept that this imagery that is so vivid to them may be different or not even exist at all in the linguistic culture of the target language.

In the world of professional translation, it is accepted that translators should be native speakers of the target language. And with good reason. Even the best professional translators find it very hard not to stick too closely to the source text. For those who are native speakers of the source text it is incredibly difficult.

Going a step further, transcreators need to practice their art for many years. For them, the ability to completely detach from the source is one of the main skills.

None of this is achievable for most untrained non-linguists who are not native speakers of the target language. At least not in any practical way that can be reliably applied in the world of commerce, where the goal is to hold on to the reader's attention long enough so that they understand what is being said and are persuaded by it. Yet it is often attempted. And readers the world over are confused by the results. Or just stop reading.
